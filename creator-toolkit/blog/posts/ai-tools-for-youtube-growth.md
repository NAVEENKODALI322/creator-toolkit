AI tools promise to save creators hours every week. Some of them genuinely do. Others just move the work around without removing it. Here's where the line actually sits.

## Where AI genuinely helps

**Titles and hooks.** Generating five or six title variations in seconds, then picking (or editing) the one that fits your video, is faster than staring at a blank field. The value isn't the final title AI gives you — it's the starting points you wouldn't have thought of yourself.

**Tags and descriptions.** These are structured, repetitive tasks with clear rules (keyword relevance, length limits, avoiding repetition). This is exactly the kind of task AI handles well, because the "creativity" isn't the point — coverage and consistency are.

**First-draft hooks for Shorts.** A strong first three seconds is formulaic more often than creators like to admit. AI is useful for generating a batch of hook options to react to, not for writing your final line.

## Where it adds noise instead of saving time

**Full scripts.** AI-written scripts tend to sound like AI — generic pacing, no personality, no callback to what your specific audience already knows about you. Editing a bad AI script often takes longer than writing a rough one yourself.

**Thumbnail copy.** Two or three words that carry your channel's specific tone don't come from a generic prompt. This is worth doing yourself.

**Anything that needs your actual voice.** If a viewer would recognize it as "sounding like you," AI drafts are a starting point at best — never the final version.

## A simple filter

Before reaching for an AI tool, ask: is this task structured and repetitive, or does it need my specific point of view? Structured and repetitive → let AI draft it, you edit. Needs your point of view → write it yourself, maybe use AI to bounce ideas off of.

Used this way, AI tools don't replace the creative work — they clear out the mechanical work around it, so there's more time left for the part only you can do.
